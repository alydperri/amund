# Opportunity Navigation

*For AI pioneers considering or pursuing a new role — what's specific to this situation that generic interview advice doesn't cover.*

---

## What Makes This Different

Generic interview prep assumes a defined role, a standard career ladder, and a candidate who needs to prove they can do a job that others have done before. None of that applies here.

You're a pioneer interviewing for a pioneer role. You built something that didn't exist. You're evaluating an org that's figuring out what it needs. The stakes are high in both directions — for you and for them. That changes almost everything about how to navigate the process.

---

## 1. Translating Work That's Hard to Name

The challenge: your most important work happened in the margins — the conversation that shifted a decision, the system you built that no one knew to ask for, the thing you figured out that your org didn't have language for yet.

Standard interview frameworks ("tell me about a time you led a project") don't fit this work well. The risk is either over-narrating (drowning the insight in context) or under-explaining (sounding vague when you were actually precise).

**The translation principle:** Start with the problem, not your role in solving it. What was broken or missing? What did the org not know it needed? Then show what changed because you existed. Let the impact do the work — your title and process are secondary.

**What to avoid:** Justifying your approach before they've asked. If you find yourself saying "I know this sounds unusual but..." stop. That's the over-explaining instinct kicking in. State what you did and what it produced. Let them ask how.

**The test:** Can you explain your most important work in two minutes to someone who doesn't share your context — without jargon, without apology, without over-qualifying? If not, practice that before any conversation.

---

## 2. Evaluating the Org During the Process

Most candidates treat interviews as one-directional — prove yourself, get the offer. In a pioneer role, you have an equal obligation to evaluate whether the conditions for success actually exist.

A pioneer role without the right conditions isn't an opportunity. It's a setup.

**What to assess:**

*Air cover.* Who is the executive sponsor for this role? What have they personally committed to in order for it to succeed? If the answer is vague, that's information.

*Appetite for ambiguity.* How does the org talk about what this role will own? If they have a precise answer, that's either a sign of clarity or a sign the role is more defined (and constrained) than it appears. If they're vague, can they tolerate that vagueness long enough for you to build something real?

*How they define success.* Ask directly: "How will you know in twelve months whether this role worked?" If they can't answer specifically, you'll be building the metrics while being held to invisible ones. That's a pattern worth recognizing.

*What happened before you.* Was there a previous person in this role? What happened to them? If it's a new role, who has been doing the work informally, and how will they respond to someone owning it formally?

*How they talk about AI internally.* Is AI a function or a feature? Do the people interviewing you understand what a learning systems or strategy function actually does, or are they pattern-matching to something adjacent? Misunderstanding at the interview stage usually gets worse after you join.

---

## 3. The "Why Leave?" Question

This is the question with the most landmines. A bad answer undermines your current role, signals instability, or sounds like you're running away from something rather than toward something.

The honest version is usually complex: you're in a good role, you've built something real, and there's a specific reason this opportunity is worth considering anyway. That complexity is worth sitting with before you're asked to compress it in real time.

**What doesn't work:**
- Criticizing your current org (even subtly)
- Overselling the opportunity before you know if it's real
- Claiming you've "done everything you can do" there — it reads as restless, not accomplished

**What does work:**
Being specific about what this opportunity offers that your current role can't. Not "more growth" — what specifically. Is it the scale? The frontier nature of the work? The chance to build in a context where AI is the core, not a layer? Say that. Specifically.

The frame that usually lands: you're not leaving something, you're moving toward something that's only become possible because of what you built. That's honest and positions your current work as foundation, not failure.

---

## 4. The Over-Explaining Instinct

Pioneer practitioners spend years defending their work — justifying the role's existence, translating their impact for audiences who don't have the frame, arguing for resources that others take for granted. That instinct is adaptive in a pioneer role.

In an interview for a frontier AI role, it can read as insecurity.

If the org is worth joining, the people interviewing you should have enough context to understand your work without extensive scaffolding. Treat them as intelligent peers, not skeptics who need to be convinced.

**Signs you're over-explaining:**
- Anticipating objections before they're raised
- Adding "I know this might sound..." qualifiers
- Repeating the same point in different words
- Defending a decision before anyone questioned it

**The adjustment:** Say the thing. Watch their reaction. Let them ask follow-up questions. Their questions will tell you what actually needs more context — and you'll give it with precision rather than covering everything preemptively.

---

## 5. Knowing Your Walkaway Before the Offer Arrives

Before you're in the room where an offer might happen, get clear on what you'd say no to. Not because you're in a strong position — your situation is your own and only you know it — but because clarity about your own conditions protects you from deciding under pressure.

Pressure changes how things feel. An offer that arrives with a deadline and excitement and flattery is harder to evaluate clearly than the same offer imagined in a quiet moment.

**Questions worth sitting with before you're in that moment:**

What would make this a clear yes, regardless of anything else?

What would make this a clear no, regardless of how good everything else seemed?

What are you most afraid of getting wrong — and is that fear based on real information or uncertainty?

If you took this role and it didn't work out in eighteen months, what would you wish you'd asked before accepting?

What does your current role become if you leave? Is there unfinished work that matters to you — and what does walking away from it mean?

There are no right answers to these. The point is to have thought through them before you need to decide quickly.
