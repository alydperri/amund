# Making Work Visible

*For when your impact is real but no one can see it — or you can't prove it yet.*

---

## The Visibility Problem

Pioneer roles have a structural visibility problem: the most important work is often the work that prevented something bad, enabled something that got credited elsewhere, or happened in conversations that left no paper trail.

This is not a communication problem. It's a documentation and framing problem. You have to build the artifact that shows the work, because the work itself doesn't surface automatically.

---

## Activity vs. Impact

The most common mistake: making activity visible instead of impact.

**Activity visible:** "I ran 12 workshops, built 3 tools, and met with 8 teams this quarter."

**Impact visible:** "The sales team reduced onboarding time by 3 weeks using the AI workflow we built together. Here's what that cost previously and what it costs now."

Activity says you were busy. Impact says the org is different because you exist.

If you can't point to something that changed, that's the real problem — not visibility.

---

## When You Don't Have Metrics Yet

New roles rarely have metrics. You have to create them before you need them — not after leadership asks.

**Work backward from what your company cares about:**
- Revenue? Show how your work supports a revenue-generating team
- Speed? Quantify time saved or cycles reduced
- Quality? Show error rates, rework, or decision confidence
- Scale? Show how something that required a specialist can now be done by anyone

**Use proxies when direct metrics don't exist:**
- Before/after comparisons (even rough ones)
- Testimonials from stakeholders framed around business outcomes
- Adoption numbers as a proxy for value (with caveats)
- Qualitative signal from leadership that tracks back to your work

**The key move:** Establish a baseline before you start. Without a before, there's no after.

---

## The Quarterly Role Snapshot

Every quarter, write a one-page summary of your role's impact. Not a brag sheet — a record. Include:

1. What the role owned this quarter
2. What shipped, happened, or changed
3. What it was before vs. after (even roughly)
4. What's still in progress and why
5. What you learned about where the role should focus next

Do this whether or not anyone asks for it. It compounds. In six months, you have a body of evidence. In a year, you have a case.

---

## Making Your Work Legible to People Who Don't Understand It

The hardest version of visibility: translating AI work to audiences who don't have the frame for it.

**The translation formula:**
- Start with their problem, not your solution
- Show the before state in their language
- Show the after state in measurable terms
- Let them ask how

**What to avoid:**
- Technical jargon before credibility
- Leading with the tool instead of the outcome
- Assuming they care about AI — they care about their problem

**The test:** Can your most skeptical stakeholder repeat back what your work does and why it matters? If not, the translation isn't done.
