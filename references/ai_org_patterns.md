# AI Org Patterns

*Common ways organizations structure AI roles and where they tend to break down. Useful for diagnosing your situation and finding where your leverage actually lives.*

---

## Why "Everyone Owns AI" Always Creates a Vacuum

When AI responsibility is distributed across an organization without coordination, a predictable pattern emerges:

- Everyone is responsible → no one is accountable
- Every team has an AI initiative → no one knows what the others are doing
- Leadership expects AI progress → no one knows who to ask
- The vacuum exists → someone fills it

The pioneer role is usually the person who fills the vacuum. The problem is that filling a vacuum without formal mandate means you're operating in contested space. That's not bad — it's the job. But it requires a different approach than a role with clear authority.

---

## Common AI Role Structures (and Their Failure Modes)

### The Central AI Team
A dedicated team owns AI strategy and tooling for the whole organization.

*Works when:* The team has executive sponsorship and a clear mandate.

*Fails when:* The team becomes a bottleneck, loses touch with business unit reality, or gets defined as "AI for AI's sake" rather than AI for business outcomes.

*If you're adjacent to this:* Your leverage is being closer to the business than they are. You translate strategy into practice.

---

### The Embedded AI Practitioner
One person per function or business unit responsible for AI in their domain.

*Works when:* There's a coordination layer across embedded practitioners and shared standards exist.

*Fails when:* Each embedded practitioner works in isolation, reinventing the wheel, with no path to institutional leverage.

*If this is you:* The biggest risk is becoming a local resource rather than an organizational one. Deliberately cross-pollinate your work.

---

### The AI Champion
A senior leader who advocates for AI adoption without a dedicated team.

*Works when:* The champion has credibility and can unlock resources.

*Fails when:* The champion has no execution support and AI enthusiasm outpaces AI delivery. Everything becomes the AI practitioner's problem to solve.

*If you support a champion:* Protect them from over-promising. Help them say "we're testing" instead of "we're rolling out."

---

### The AI Task Force
A cross-functional group convened to drive AI adoption.

*Works when:* The task force has clear decision rights and a time-bound mandate.

*Fails when:* The task force has no authority to actually change anything. Meetings happen; nothing ships.

*If you're in or adjacent to one:* Find the person on the task force who can actually remove blockers. Work through them.

---

## The Structural Pattern to Watch For

In almost every organization, there's a gap between:
- Where AI decisions are made (senior leadership)
- Where AI is implemented (individual teams)

The pioneer role often sits in that gap. That gap is both the source of your leverage and the source of your frustration. You can see both sides; neither side fully sees you.

The goal is to make that position a feature, not a bug — being the connective tissue is a real role. But it only works if both sides feel served by it.
