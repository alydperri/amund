# Managing Up

*For when your manager hasn't done your job, can't fully evaluate your output, and may not know what you need to succeed.*

---

## The Core Dynamic

In a pioneer role, the manager relationship has a structural asymmetry that doesn't exist in established roles: your manager almost certainly hasn't done your job. They may not know what good looks like, what the real obstacles are, or how to evaluate whether you're succeeding.

This isn't a failure of your manager. It's a predictable feature of a first-of-kind role. Working with it — rather than waiting for them to figure it out — is part of the job.

---

## What Your Manager Needs From You That They Won't Ask For

**A frame for your work.**
If your manager can't explain what you do and why it matters, they can't advocate for you, protect your scope, or evaluate your progress accurately. You need to give them that frame — repeatedly, in different contexts — until it's theirs.

**Visibility into obstacles, not just progress.**
Most people report what's going well. Your manager also needs to know what's slowing you down and what they could do about it. If you never surface blockers, they assume none exist.

**A way to say yes to things on your behalf.**
When stakeholders ask your manager about your work, they need enough context to answer confidently. Give them the talking points. Make it easy for them to represent you accurately.

**Clear signals when you need their intervention.**
You're likely highly self-sufficient. Your manager may not realize when a situation has escalated past what self-sufficiency can solve. Don't wait until you're frustrated — name it when you need them specifically.

---

## When Your Manager Doesn't Understand Your Work

This is common and rarely malicious. The options:

**Educate proactively.** Don't wait for them to ask. Brief them regularly on what you're doing, what it requires, and what success looks like. Assume they need more context than they're asking for.

**Translate into their language.** Your manager cares about what their leadership cares about. Understand that frame and map your work to it explicitly. Don't make them do the translation.

**Create artifacts they can use.** A one-pager on your role's scope and impact. A quarterly summary of what shipped and what it produced. Something they can pull up when they need to represent your work.

**Find what they do understand and build from there.** Most managers who don't understand a specialist's work deeply still understand business outcomes. Start there.

---

## Alignment Conversations Worth Having Proactively

Don't wait for a performance review or a crisis to have these:

- **What does success look like in 6 months?** Get explicit alignment — then revisit it. The definition may shift; you want to know when it does.
- **What would concern you about how this role is going?** Understanding your manager's failure modes for you lets you avoid them or address them early.
- **What decisions can I make independently vs. what should involve you?** Ambiguity here costs both of you. Make it explicit.
- **How do you prefer to hear about problems?** Some managers want early, rough signals. Others want you to come with a solution. Know which you're working with.

---

## The Difference Between Venting and Informing

When something is hard, the temptation is to surface the difficulty without a clear ask. This can read as complaining — even when it's legitimate.

Before raising a problem with your manager:
- What do you want them to do about it?
- Is this information, a request for help, or a decision you need from them?

Being clear about the type of conversation you're initiating makes it easier for them to respond usefully.
