# Pioneer Role Playbook

*The moves that matter when you're building a role that didn't exist before.*

---

## The Foundational Truth

You made this role, or it found you because no one else stepped up. Either way, it's yours. No one is going to:
- Define success for you
- Tell you what to prioritize
- Advocate for your comp without data you provide
- Protect your scope when other teams expand into it
- Notice your impact unless you make it visible

This isn't a complaint about your organization. It's the operating reality of a first-of-kind role. The playbook that follows is built around it.

---

## Writing Your Own Definition of Success

If you don't define what good looks like, someone else will — usually using the wrong frame.

**Do this early:**

1. Write down what the role should accomplish in 12 months, in business terms your company cares about
2. Identify 3-5 specific outcomes that would prove the role was worth creating
3. Share it with your manager and key stakeholders — not for approval, for alignment
4. Revisit it quarterly and update it based on what you've learned

The document matters less than the habit. People who operate in undefined roles need to write their own north star and navigate by it.

---

## Building a Role Board of Directors

You don't have a career ladder. You have a small set of people who know what you do, believe in what you're building, and will tell you the truth.

Identify 3-4 people who:
- Understand the domain (AI, learning, or both)
- Have no stake in flattering you
- Are willing to be honest about what's working and what isn't
- Can open doors you can't open yourself

These aren't mentors in the traditional sense. They're sounding boards, truth-tellers, and connectors. They should know they play this role.

---

## When to Expand vs. Consolidate Scope

The pressure in a pioneer role is almost always to take on more. More problems, more teams, more use cases. This can build influence — or it can spread you so thin you lose credibility everywhere.

**Expand scope when:**
- You've delivered something concrete in your current scope
- The new scope is adjacent, not orthogonal
- You have the capacity to do it well
- Someone with authority has explicitly asked for it

**Consolidate scope when:**
- You're spread across more than you can demonstrate impact on
- You're becoming the default owner of problems that aren't yours
- Your core mandate is unclear to the people who matter
- You're busy but not impactful

The test: can you point to three things in your current scope that are measurably better because of your work? If not, consolidate before expanding.

---

## Arguing for Your Own Comp

In a first-of-kind role, your company may not know what to pay you. HR may classify it as a variant of something familiar. You may get promoted into the new role at the pay of the old one.

This is common. It requires you to make the case explicitly and with data.

**The comp argument has four parts:**

1. **Scope** — what the role actually owns and who it serves
2. **Market** — what comparable roles pay externally
3. **Internal precedent** — what similar-level roles at your company pay, especially other AI-focused ones
4. **Impact** — what has changed because this role exists

Don't wait for your company to figure this out on their own. Build the document. Request the meeting. Make it easy for your manager and HR to advocate for you by giving them the argument.

---

## The Humble Experimentation Posture

The instinct in a pioneer role is to sell certainty — you built something no one asked for, so you feel you have to justify it constantly. This often reads as overconfidence to the people you most need to trust you.

The posture that actually builds trust:

- Lead with the problem, not the solution
- Offer a test, not a rollout
- Report what you learned, not just what worked
- Invite skeptics in early rather than proving them wrong later

"Let's run a pilot and see what we learn" is not a weak position. In organizations navigating AI uncertainty, it's often the most credible thing you can say.
